Signal Processing

Before analyzing your EEG data, you need to understand what you're looking at. This page introduces spectrograms — the core visualization tool for EEG frequency analysis — and explains how to read them.

What is a Spectrogram?

Raw EEG data is a voltage signal that varies over time. While it's possible to look at that raw waveform directly, the most informative way to analyze EEG is to ask: which frequencies are present, and when? A spectrogram answers exactly that question.

A spectrogram is produced by dividing the recording into short overlapping windows, computing the frequency content of each window using a Fast Fourier Transform (FFT), and then stacking those frequency snapshots side by side. The result is a two-dimensional image where you can see how spectral power changes across the entire recording at once.

Spectrogram anatomy

Schematic spectrogram. Time runs left to right; frequency runs bottom to top. Color encodes spectral power — warm colors indicate high power, cool colors indicate low power. The bright region around 8–13 Hz represents elevated alpha activity.

How to Read a Spectrogram

Once you know what the three dimensions represent, spectrograms become straightforward to interpret.

⟶
X-Axis — Time

Runs left to right. Each column of pixels represents one short time window. The full width spans the entire recording duration.

↑
Y-Axis — Frequency

Runs bottom (0 Hz) to top (100 Hz). Higher positions on the spectrogram correspond to faster neural oscillations.

🎨
Color — Power

Encodes how strong the signal is at that frequency and time. Use the color scale shown below to interpret intensity.

When the Z-score normalization option is enabled, each frequency band is normalized independently to its own mean and standard deviation. This makes it easier to compare activity across bands that have very different baseline power levels — particularly useful for comparing low-frequency (delta, theta) and high-frequency (beta, gamma) bands side-by-side.

EEG Frequency Bands

Neuroscientists divide the EEG spectrum into named bands. Each band is associated with distinct brain states and cognitive functions. The dashed horizontal lines you see in the spectrogram tool mark the boundaries between these bands.

Band Frequency Associated states
δ Delta 0.5 – 4 Hz Deep sleep, unconsciousness, severe pathology
θ Theta 4 – 8 Hz Drowsiness, memory encoding, focused attention, meditation
α Alpha 8 – 13 Hz Relaxed wakefulness, eyes closed, idling visual cortex
β Beta 13 – 30 Hz Active thinking, problem-solving, motor control, alertness
γ Low Gamma 30 – 70 Hz High-level cognition, sensory binding, conscious perception
γ High Gamma 70 – 100 Hz Local cortical processing, active motor areas, high-frequency noise

Keep in mind that these boundaries are conventional, not sharp functional divisions. Real neural activity is continuous, and power often transitions gradually across band borders. The labels are a useful shorthand for communication — not rigid physiological categories.

Interpreting Your Data

When you open your recording in the EEG Data Preview tool, here are the key patterns to look for:

  • 1 Look for the alpha peak. Alpha (8–13 Hz) is the most prominent rhythm in a typical resting EEG. When eyes are closed, you should see a distinct bright band in that frequency range. When eyes open or a cognitive task begins, this band should visibly dim — this is called "alpha desynchronization" or the "alpha blocking" response.
  • 2 Watch for task-related changes. Mental effort tends to increase beta and theta power while suppressing alpha. If your recording includes different task conditions, switch to View All mode and look for these shifts as horizontal bands that brighten or dim at specific time points.
  • 3 Use key press markers to time-lock events. If you have an events file (the .txt file exported alongside your recording), load it with the LOAD EVENTS button. Vertical dashed lines will appear at each event time, letting you see what changed in the spectrum immediately before and after key moments.
  • 4 Zoom into regions of interest. In View All mode, click and drag across the spectrogram to zoom into any time window. This is especially useful for examining brief events or transitions in detail. The Export JPG button captures the current view — including the zoom window and any event markers — exactly as shown.
  • 5 Identify artifacts. Broadband vertical streaks (power across all frequencies at one moment) often indicate movement artifacts. Strong 50 or 60 Hz lines indicate electrical interference from the power line. Unusually high delta power in an awake participant may reflect slow head movements or electrode drift.
