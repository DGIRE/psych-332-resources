📚 What Is the Module 1 Practicum?

The Module 1 Practicum is a research report in which you investigate three interconnected topics using real neuroscience datasets. You will write three main sections, each containing three figure-based subsections plus a statement of work:

  1. Section 1 — Brain Area Report: Anatomy, receptor gene expression, and cognitive function of a chosen brain region.
  2. Section 2 — fMRI Function Report: A Neurosynth keyword function’s brain-wide representation and its connection to transcriptomic and connectomic data.
  3. Section 3 — Receptor Report: A specific receptor’s structure, brain-wide expression, and relationship to fMRI functions.

Each subsection requires one figure with a detailed caption and a written analysis that incorporates both the course datasets (gene expression, DTI, fMRI) and outside scientific literature.

🏆 How You Are Graded

The practicum is worth 40 points total. Each of the 9 figure subsections is scored on three criteria:

Criterion Points What It Means
Comprehension 1.5 You correctly understand and explain the science. Claims are accurate and supported by evidence.
Clearness 1.5 Your writing and figures are easy to follow. Figures have labeled panels, and captions walk the reader through each panel.
Creativeness 1.0 You go beyond the basics—interesting comparisons, unexpected connections, or novel visual presentations.

That gives 4 points per subsection × 9 subsections = 36 points, plus 4 points for submitting statements of work on time. The statements of work for all three sections are due before the final practicum.

Tip: The “creativeness” point rewards intellectual curiosity. Ask yourself: “What would surprise the reader?” or “What connection hasn’t been made yet?”

📝 Writing Statements of Work (Subsection 0)

Every section starts with a Statement of Work (Subsection 0). This is a 1–2 paragraph preview describing exactly what you will investigate in that section. Think of it as a contract with yourself.

What a Good Statement Includes

  • The specific brain area, function, or receptor you chose and why.
  • The data sources you will use (Allen transcriptomic data, Neurosynth fMRI maps, DTI connectivity).
  • The specific questions or comparisons you plan to address in each subsection.
  • Any outside topics you plan to connect to (disorders, drugs, evolution).
Example: “I will investigate the CACNA1E expression in the amygdala. I will focus on its role in emotional processing and anxiety disorders. Additionally, I will focus on synaptic plasticity, comparing CACNA1E expression in the amygdala to its expression in the hippocampus.”
Important: Once submitted, do not alter your statement of work. Include it as-is in your final practicum. It is graded on timeliness, not on whether your final work changed direction.

📊 Creating Effective Figures

Each subsection centers on one multi-panel figure. The best figures combine course data visualizations with outside images from the scientific literature.

Figure Construction

  • Use labeled panels (A, B, C, D…). Put a clear letter label in the corner of each sub-image so your caption can reference them.
  • Mix data types. A strong figure might pair a brain map from the Colab notebook (panel A) with a bar chart of expression data (panel B) and a diagram from a published paper (panel C).
  • Annotate directly on the figure. Add arrows, labels, and color highlights to draw the reader’s eye to the key finding.
  • Use colored borders or background tints to visually group related panels.

Writing Figure Captions

  • Start with a bold title that states the figure’s main point (e.g., “Figure 1: Amygdala Anatomy”).
  • Walk through each panel by letter: “(A) shows… (B) illustrates…”
  • Don’t just describe what’s visible—interpret the data. What does this panel mean for the question you’re investigating?
  • Cite every outside image or dataset with an in-text citation (Author et al., Year).
Tip: Look at the example practicum’s figures. Notice how each panel serves a distinct purpose and the caption weaves them into a single narrative about the brain region, function, or receptor.

📑 Section-by-Section Guide

Section 1: Brain Area Report

You pick one brain region and write three subsections about its anatomy, gene expression, and cognitive role.

Introduce your brain area and use the DSI connectivity data to explore its anatomical connections.

  • What other brain regions is it structurally connected to? Use the DTI/DSI visualizations to show this.
  • How is the region defined anatomically? What cell types does it contain?
  • How can this area vary across individuals?
  • What are the implications of its connections for its function?
Strong approach: Pair a brain map showing the region (from the Colab notebook) with a published DSI or DTI image showing its white matter tracts, then discuss how those connections support cognition.

Use Allen Institute transcriptomic data to investigate which receptor genes are highly or lowly expressed in your region.

  • Highlight 2–3 genes with notably high or low expression and explain their relevance.
  • Connect expression patterns to neurological disorders associated with the region.
  • Are there drugs (pharmaceutical or recreational) that target these receptors?
  • How does your region’s expression compare to neighboring regions?
Strong approach: Use the gene expression bar chart from the Colab, annotate the high/low genes with color-coded arrows, and pair with a published figure showing the receptor’s mechanism of action.

Investigate how your brain area participates in cognition using Neurosynth fMRI keyword maps.

  • Which cognitive functions activate your region? Show fMRI maps for 1–2 key functions.
  • What other regions does it work with to execute these functions?
  • What happens when the region is disrupted (lesion studies, disorders)?
Strong approach: Show fMRI activation maps for two related functions side by side, with your region highlighted, then discuss how they converge on that area.

Section 2: fMRI Function Report

You pick one Neurosynth keyword function and explore how the brain represents it.

Describe the function, how it is used in daily life, and how it is measured with fMRI.

  • Look up your keyword on neurosynth.org to find how many studies contribute to the map.
  • Explain how fMRI works at a conceptual level (BOLD signal, hemodynamic response).
  • What types of experimental tasks are used to study this function in the scanner?
  • What are limitations of the Neurosynth dataset for this function?

Create an fMRI brain map of your function and analyze which regions drive it.

  • Which regions show the highest activation? Use the bar plot and brain map from the Colab.
  • How do different brain areas coordinate to produce this function?
  • What neurological disorders affect this function, and how does that appear in fMRI data?

This is the most challenging subsection. You attempt to bridge fMRI functional data with gene expression and white matter connectivity.

  • Transcriptomic analysis: Do the regions supporting your function share receptor expression profiles? If you designed a drug to modulate the function, what receptor would you target?
  • Connectomic analysis: Are the regions supporting your function structurally connected to each other?
  • Be honest about limitations—combining data sources is inherently imperfect and acknowledging this is part of the grade.
Note: This subsection rewards honest scientific reasoning more than clean answers. It is okay if the data don’t align perfectly—explain why they might not.

Section 3: Receptor Report

You pick one receptor gene and investigate its biology, brain-wide expression, and relationship to fMRI functions.

  • What is the receptor’s molecular structure and how was it determined?
  • What gene family does it belong to? How ancient and conserved is that family?
  • Which neurotransmitter activates it? Are there known drugs that interact with it?
  • What behavioral or perceptual effects come from modulating it?
  • Use the Colab notebook to map where the receptor is expressed across the brain.
  • Which regions show high vs. low expression? Does the pattern correlate with evolutionary age of structures?
  • Does expression overlap with regions associated with known neurological disorders?
  • Are there other family members with similar patterns?
  • Compare the receptor’s expression map to Neurosynth function maps. Do they overlap?
  • If you suppressed or upregulated this receptor with a drug, which functions might be impacted?
  • What does outside research say about drugs that target this receptor?

✍️ Writing Best Practices

Scientific Writing Style

  • Be specific, not vague. Instead of “this gene is important,” say “CACNA1E is highly expressed in the amygdala (expression score: 0.88), where it contributes to calcium channel function and efficient synaptic transmission.”
  • Use data as evidence. Every claim should reference either a course dataset visualization or a published source.
  • Cite as you go. Use in-text citations in (Author, Year) format. Don’t save all citations for the end.
  • Stay in the third person for scientific claims: “The amygdala is implicated in…” rather than “I think the amygdala does…” (first person is fine in the Statement of Work).

Structuring Each Subsection

  1. Opening sentence: State the main point of the subsection.
  2. Body: Walk through the figure panels, integrating outside literature to explain what the data show.
  3. Closing sentence: Tie the subsection back to the broader theme of your section.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Don’t just describe the figure—interpret it. “The bar chart shows high expression” is description; “High SCN5A expression in the amygdala may underlie its role in fear and anxiety response via sodium channel modulation” is interpretation.
  • Don’t ignore limitations. Especially in the cross-data sections (Section 2 Sub 3, Section 3 Sub 3), acknowledge where datasets don’t align and explain why.
  • Don’t forget to label figure panels (A, B, C) and reference them in the caption.
  • Don’t recycle the same outside source for every claim—use a variety of references.

✅ Pre-Submission Checklist

  • Statements of work (Subsection 0) are included for all three sections and were not modified after initial submission
  • Each of the 9 subsections has exactly one multi-panel figure with labeled panels (A, B, C…)
  • Every figure has a descriptive caption that references each panel by letter
  • Course data visualizations (gene expression, DTI, fMRI maps) are incorporated into figures
  • Outside scientific literature is cited in (Author, Year) format throughout
  • Section 1 uses DSI/DTI data for anatomy, Allen data for gene expression, and Neurosynth for function
  • Section 2 includes a Neurosynth function map and attempts to connect to transcriptomics and connectomics
  • Section 3 describes the receptor, maps its expression, and connects it to fMRI function data
  • Limitations of combining data sources are discussed where appropriate
