Finding the Major Axes of Variation in High-Dimensional Brain Data

Overview

What Is PCA?

Principal Component Analysis is a mathematical technique that takes a dataset with many variables — such as the expression levels of thousands of genes measured across dozens of brain regions — and identifies the directions along which the data varies the most. These directions are called principal components (PCs). The first principal component (PC1) captures the single largest axis of variation in the data, PC2 captures the second largest axis that is perpendicular to PC1, and so on.

PCA works by computing the covariance matrix of the data and then finding its eigenvectors and eigenvalues. Each eigenvector defines a principal component direction, and its corresponding eigenvalue tells you how much of the total variance in the data that component explains. PCA then rotates the coordinate system so that the new axes (the principal components) align with these directions of maximum variance.

The result is a new set of coordinates for each data point. Instead of being described by thousands of gene expression values, each brain region can now be described by its position along just the first few principal components — the ones that capture the most variance. This makes it possible to visualize high-dimensional data in two or three dimensions while retaining as much of the original information as possible.

Core Concepts

Key Concepts

Variance explained

Each principal component captures a fraction of the total variance in the dataset. PC1 always captures the most, PC2 the second most, and so on. If PC1 explains 45% of the variance and PC2 explains 20%, then a scatter plot of PC1 vs. PC2 represents 65% of the total variation in the data. The remaining 35% is spread across the higher components. A scree plot shows the variance explained by each component in descending order, helping you judge how many components are needed to capture the essential structure of the data.

Loadings

Each principal component is a weighted combination of the original variables. These weights are called loadings. A loading tells you how much a specific original variable (for example, the expression of a particular gene) contributes to that component. If a gene has a large positive loading on PC1, it means that gene's expression level is strongly associated with the direction of maximum variation. Genes with large loadings (positive or negative) on the same component tend to co-vary together across brain regions.

In the context of brain data, inspecting the top loadings on PC1 can reveal which genes, connectivity features, or functional activations drive the largest organizational distinction among brain regions — often the cortical versus subcortical divide.

Scores

The score of a data point on a principal component is its coordinate in the new rotated space. When you plot brain regions on a PCA scatter plot, you are plotting their scores on two components. Regions with similar scores on PC1 and PC2 have similar overall profiles in the original high-dimensional data. The spread of scores along a component reflects how much the data points vary along that axis.

Biplots

A biplot overlays the scores (data points) and the loadings (variable arrows) on the same plot. Each arrow represents an original variable, pointing in the direction of its loading. Brain regions that lie in the direction an arrow points tend to have high values for that variable. Biplots let you simultaneously see which brain regions group together and which original variables are responsible for that grouping.

Orthogonality

Principal components are always orthogonal (perpendicular) to each other. This means they capture completely independent patterns of variation. The information in PC1 has zero overlap with the information in PC2. This is a mathematical guarantee that makes PCA components non-redundant — each component tells you something genuinely new about the structure of the data.

Comparison

PCA vs. UMAP

PCA and UMAP are both dimensionality reduction methods, but they work very differently and have complementary strengths.

PCA is linear. It can only find straight-line axes of variation. If the structure of your data lies along curved surfaces or complex manifolds in high-dimensional space, PCA will miss it or distort it. UMAP can capture nonlinear relationships because it works by preserving local neighborhood structure rather than global linear axes.

PCA is deterministic. Given the same data, PCA always produces the same result. There are no random seeds, no stochastic optimization, and no hyperparameters to tune. This makes PCA results perfectly reproducible and removes the need for stability checks across multiple runs.

PCA preserves global structure quantitatively. Distances and angles in a PCA plot have precise mathematical meanings tied to variance and correlation. UMAP preserves local neighborhoods well but distorts global distances. If you need to make quantitative statements about how different two brain regions are, PCA is the better choice.

PCA components are interpretable. You can inspect the loadings to understand exactly what biological variables drive each axis. UMAP dimensions have no such interpretable meaning — the x and y axes are arbitrary embedding coordinates.

When to use which: Use PCA when you need interpretable axes, quantitative distances, or want to know which variables drive the major patterns. Use UMAP when you want to visualize complex, nonlinear cluster structure or when PCA's first two components capture too little variance to be informative. Best practice is to use both and compare: patterns that emerge in both PCA and UMAP are the most trustworthy.
Critical Caveats

Common Misinterpretations to Avoid

Do not assume PCA components correspond to single biological processes. A principal component is a weighted mixture of all original variables. PC1 might reflect a combination of cell-type composition, developmental origin, and metabolic activity all at once. It is rarely the case that a single clean biological story explains an entire component.
Do not ignore the variance explained. If PC1 and PC2 together explain only 15% of the total variance, the scatter plot is showing you a small fraction of the data's structure. Important groupings may exist in higher components that are not visible in the standard 2D plot.
PCA is sensitive to the scale of variables. If one variable has values ranging from 0 to 10,000 while another ranges from 0 to 1, the large-range variable will dominate the principal components regardless of its biological importance. This is why data is typically z-score normalized before PCA — to put all variables on the same scale. Without normalization, PCA results can be misleading.
Outliers can distort PCA. Because PCA maximizes variance, a single extreme data point can pull an entire principal component toward itself. Always check for outliers before interpreting PCA results, and consider whether extreme values reflect genuine biology or measurement artifacts.
The sign of a component is arbitrary. Multiplying all loadings and scores on a component by −1 produces an equally valid solution. If PC1 has positive loadings for cortical genes, an equivalent analysis might show negative loadings for those same genes with all the scores flipped. The relationships between data points remain identical — only the direction of the axis is reversed.
Interpretation Guide

How to Read a PCA Plot: A Step-by-Step Approach

  1. Check the variance explained. Look at the axis labels: they should tell you how much variance PC1 and PC2 capture. If the two components together explain less than about 30% of the total variance, the plot is showing you only a partial picture and you should explore higher components as well.
  2. Identify clusters and gradients. Look for groups of brain regions that cluster together. Also look for continuous gradients where regions are spread along an axis rather than forming discrete clumps. Both patterns are biologically meaningful.
  3. Interpret the axes using loadings. Examine which original variables have the largest loadings on PC1 and PC2. These are the variables that drive the separation you see in the plot. For gene expression data, the top-loading genes on PC1 often relate to the cortical vs. subcortical distinction.
  4. Check for outliers. Brain regions that sit far from all others may have unusual profiles. Determine whether they represent genuine biological outliers (like the cerebellum, which has a highly distinct gene expression signature) or possible data quality issues.
  5. Examine higher components. PC3, PC4, and beyond may reveal additional structure that is invisible in the PC1 vs. PC2 plot. Plotting PC1 vs. PC3 or PC2 vs. PC3 can expose groupings that the first two components miss.
  6. Cross-reference with other methods. Compare PCA groupings with UMAP clusters and dendrogram branches. Patterns that are consistent across methods are the most reliable.
Your Analysis Plots

Reading the Biplot and Loadings Bar Chart

On the Data Analysis page, two auxiliary plots appear below the main PCA scatter plot. These help you understand why brain regions separate the way they do.

The Biplot

The biplot overlays two layers of information on a single plot. The points are the brain regions (or features), identical to the main PCA scatter plot. The gold arrows represent the top loading variables — these are the original measurements (genes, connectivity targets, or fMRI functions) that contribute most strongly to PC1 and PC2.

Each arrow starts at the origin and points in the direction of that variable's loadings on PC1 (horizontal) and PC2 (vertical). A longer arrow means that variable has a larger influence on the plotted components. Brain regions that lie in the direction an arrow points tend to have high values for that variable relative to other regions.

To interpret a biplot, look for arrows that point toward clusters of brain regions. If an arrow labeled "SLC17A7" points toward cortical regions, that gene is more highly expressed in cortex. If two arrows point in similar directions, those variables tend to co-vary across brain regions. Arrows pointing in opposite directions indicate variables with an inverse relationship.

Scaling note: The arrow lengths are scaled to fit on the same axes as the scores. This means you can compare arrow directions and relative lengths, but you should not directly compare an arrow length to a score distance. The biplot shows patterns of association, not absolute magnitudes.

The Loadings Bar Chart

The loadings bar chart shows the top variables ranked by their absolute loading on PC1. Each bar extends to the left (negative loading) or right (positive loading). Variables with large positive loadings on PC1 are highest in brain regions with large positive PC1 scores, and lowest in regions with large negative PC1 scores. Negative-loading variables follow the opposite pattern.

This chart answers a direct question: which specific variables drive the primary axis of variation? For gene expression data, the top PC1 loadings typically include genes with strong cortical-versus-subcortical expression gradients. For DTI connectivity, the top loadings reveal which connection targets most strongly differentiate brain regions along the major organizational axis.

When writing your practicum report, the loadings bar chart is a key resource for identifying which biological variables underlie the groupings you observe. Naming specific top-loading genes or connections strengthens your analysis by connecting statistical patterns to biological function.

Suggested Activities

Open the Setosa tool and work through these exercises to build your intuition about PCA:

  1. Observe the default 2D example. Notice the red line (PC1) passing through the direction of greatest spread. The blue projections show how each point maps onto PC1. Hover over individual points to see their original position and their projected position on the component.
  2. Drag a point far away from the cluster. Watch how the principal component axis rotates to accommodate the outlier. This demonstrates why PCA is sensitive to outliers — a single extreme value can shift the direction of maximum variance.
  3. Create a tight circular cluster. Drag all points into a roughly circular arrangement. Notice that PC1 and PC2 now explain similar amounts of variance, and the component directions become somewhat arbitrary. This shows that PCA is most useful when data has a clear directional spread.
  4. Stretch the data into an elongated ellipse. Arrange points in a long, narrow shape. PC1 will align with the long axis, capturing most of the variance, while PC2 captures the small amount of perpendicular spread. This is the ideal case for PCA — most information can be represented along a single axis.
  5. Explore the 3D example. Switch to 3D mode and rotate the view. Observe how PCA finds a 2D plane that captures the most variance. Think about how this relates to your brain data analysis: you have thousands of gene expression dimensions being reduced to just two principal components.
Connection to your practicum: When you see brain regions spread along PC1 in your analysis, the same principle is at work — PC1 has found the single direction in gene expression space along which brain regions differ the most. The Setosa tool lets you see this process with 2–3 variables; your practicum data applies the same math to thousands.
