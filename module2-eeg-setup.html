Module 2 — Step 1

EEG Setup & Recording

Before recording brain waves, we'll build intuition by first recording from the heart and the eyes. Each recording type uses the same electrodes and software — and each one reveals something new about bioelectric signals.

Progression: ECG → EOG → EEG  ·  We start with signals that are large and easy to see, then work toward the subtler signals of the brain. This order builds both your technical skill and your intuition for what a clean neural recording looks like.
♥
ECG / EKG Electrocardiography

What you're recording

The heart is powered by coordinated waves of electrical depolarization. Each heartbeat generates a characteristic waveform — the P wave (atrial contraction), the QRS complex (ventricular contraction), and the T wave (ventricular repolarization) — that can be captured with electrodes placed on the skin.

ECG signals are large (millivolt range), rhythmic, and highly stereotyped, making them ideal for learning electrode placement and signal acquisition before moving to more delicate recordings.

EOG Electrooculography

What you're recording

The eye maintains a standing corneo-retinal potential: the cornea (front) is positively charged relative to the retina (back). When you move your eyes, you rotate this electrical dipole, shifting the voltage measured by electrodes placed at your temples.

EOG signals are smaller than ECG but still much larger than EEG, and they have a direct, intuitive relationship to movement. This makes EOG excellent practice for recognizing clean signals — and for understanding the eye-movement artifacts that contaminate EEG recordings.

EEG Electroencephalography

What you're recording

EEG captures the summed electrical activity of millions of cortical neurons. Unlike ECG and EOG, the signals are in the microvolt range — roughly 1,000× smaller than a heartbeat — so clean electrode placement and minimizing movement are critical.

Electrodes are placed on the scalp according to a standard called the 10-20 system, which positions them relative to anatomical landmarks. With the SpikerBox, you'll place a small number of electrodes on the forehead to capture activity from the frontal cortex.

💡 Why start with ECG and EOG?

Brain signals (EEG) are among the smallest bioelectric signals we can record non-invasively, which makes them easy to obscure with noise. By first recording ECG and EOG — which share the same equipment and workflow but produce much larger, more interpretable signals — you build hands-on experience with electrode placement, cable management, and SpikeRecorder before the challenge level increases.

EOG also connects directly to EEG analysis: eye movements and blinks are the most common artifact in human EEG data. Recognizing what they look like in a raw signal is an important practical skill for anyone doing electrophysiology research.
